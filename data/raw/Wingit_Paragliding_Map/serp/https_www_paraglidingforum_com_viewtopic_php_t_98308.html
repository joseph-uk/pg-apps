Simple answers first:

Quote:
Would a student be best to build experience outside of the punchy spring months


Yes.

Quote:
is it just a case of landing before it gets too mad around the peak times of the day


Yes.

Quote:
one thing that worries me is hitting a thermal that is taking off when I am coming in to land. Is that a valid concern?


Yes.

----------------------

Longer answers:

Quote:
Is there ever enough solar energy to make it super dangerous to fly?


Not enough information in the question. How good are you? How well-behaved is your glider? How familiar are you with it? How current are you? What is the underlying weather doing? Are you nice and high and in clear air?
If you've been flying regularly for a few years, you're pretty current, you're on an EN B wing that you know and love, and it's about as punchy as spring ever gets in the UK, I'm having a hard time picturing a day when it would be "super dangerous".

On the other hand, if you've just stepped up to a racing snake EN D with a 7+ aspect ratio, you've not flown all winter and there's an inversion with a shear layer and a nasty windy layer on top and punchy thermals beneath, you are going to have a bad day.

Quote:
Is a current pilot with good active flying skills fairly safe from collapses during XC


Again - depends on the wing and the day. Also - you don't necessarily need to give a monkey's about collapses. They happen, you recover, you move on. Losing 30% or 40% of your wing should wake you up and make you pay attention, but it shouldn't necessarily make you start looking for a landing field.

Avoid "spring conditions" for your first couple of seasons is probably good advice. Which generally means not taking off before (and I'm going to stick my neck out and name a figure, please don't neg if it doesn't apply to your home site...) something like 16:00. By that time there's probably still some thermals about, but they're not the rippers you'll get around lunchtime. Flying BEFORE the thermals get started is possible, of course, but when do you land? The first time you get hoofed? Then you're trying to top land (possibly), one of the most dangerous manoeuvres, right at the worst time of day to try it. Best to just stick to late afternoon.

Hitting a thermal when you're coming into land absolutely is a concern, and is certainly possible. It shouldn't be a problem, though, as long as you're alert. In fact, it can be great. Check out this flight:
http://www.xcleague.com/xc/flights/20162914.html

Specifically, step through it until you reach time index 12:13ish. You'll see I'm *low*, and sinking. I'm below 1000ft amsl, and the ground is at about 500ft there, so I'm really low and getting ready to land - I've picked a field... except there's a set of buildings right there so the MIGHT be thermal. So I'm ready. And you'll see I was lucky, there was a thermal. If there hadn't been, I had a field. But as it was, I got into a climb. Ten minutes later I'd gained 1500ft, and ten minutes after that I was banging on the local airspace ceiling at 3500'. The point is - ANY time you're coming into land you must be alert to the possibility that something like that could happen. Don't worry about it - hope for it. If there's a climb there - CLIMB! If you really need to be on the ground, climb a bit, mince around for five or ten minutes, then fly back. The thermal will most likely be gone. Or just land somewhere else. What you must NOT do is bimble towards your chosen landing field focused entirely on landing. Obviously focusing on landing IS important, but never take anything for granted. Just because YOU want to land doesn't mean the glider and weather are going to cooperate.

It's a very important thing for a beginner to understand (and not getting it has cost people I know months of recovery time from nasty injuries) - although it might feel like it, your flight is not over when you're on final approach. It's not even over when your feet are on the ground. Your flight is only over when your GLIDER is on the ground AND secured in such a way that it physically can't take off again. Up to that point, you must be alert to the possibility that the weather is going to pick you up, so you need to be piloting, actively, and ready for whatever the sky can throw at you until the glider is literally in a ball.

2 more things to add to Blake's post:

1. Doing an SIV course will make most of your questions obsolete since you will feel comfortable enough not to be concerned and able to judge "how bad" it is at any given time...
2. "Thermal during landing": If it is active, come in with shoulder high brakes... If a thermal hits you last second (happens with some regularity, nothing special) put your hands up and if you leave a thermal brake a little more.
With any experience, you'll simply start to feel that and do it automatically... Personally, once I'm on final approach, out of my harness and with feet dangling, I have 1 goal alone (if active/turbulent): Feel the glider and be hyper-vigilant to catch any monkey-business in a split-second!
The horror videos you are likely referring to are all of dudes that either react very wrong, or don't react at all... Paragliding is not for ham-fisted brutes. You need to feel and be aware of whats going on.

"Paragliding is the art of anticipating the invisible."

MartyMack wrote:

I was wondering about how strong the thermals are in U.K.

Is there ever enough solar energy to make it super dangerous to fly? Like you see the really strong


'Strong' isn't necessarily quite what you think it is as a new pilot. You can have a day with good climbs, averaging > 4 m/s and if the edges are smooth so entering a thermal goes + 0.5, + 1, + 2... until you find the core and the same in reverse as you exit, then it's brilliant flying and you crank the wing over in a steady turn and just watch the ground drop away.

Those same 4 m/s climbs on a sharp-edged day, where you can suddenly go from -4 to +4 and back again will be very hard work. Imagine a strong, sharp edged thermal small enough that only one side of your wing is in it - that's a 50% collapse waiting to happen, or at least a bouncy ride with a lot of active flying until you manage to get the whole glider in there (or fall out and then fly away from that area for a while ). I've walked off a UK hill with some properly good pilots before now, when the wind wasn't so bad but the seagulls were having an absolute roller-coaster and you'd have been brave or crazy to put a glider up in it.

The reason spring needs care is that the thermals are often both strong and small. On a good day in June or July, you might get the same peak climb rates but everything calms down a bit. Those days in summer are rarer - you get more RASP 5* XC forecasts in April/May - but need the experience to handle the glider as the air pushes it around.

Peak climb rates in the UK do tend to be less than in hot countries and in high mountains with strong sun and cold air, where > 8 m/s isn't that uncommon. Those kinds of climbs aren't unheard of in the UK but you're unlikely to blunder into one.

My first wing was also an Ion (4), so I have some thoughts on that:
The Ion tends to unload and roll in the wingtip first. Almost as an indicator toward stronger conditions...

I remember a pretty early flight in spring (Alps...) with thermals peaking 9m/s at times. I was a little scared, but mainly because it had never been anywhere near as strong before!
Ion looked after me, though. I had many of those "roll in's", but no "proper" collapses at all.

A lot of it comes down to how "actively" you fly, catching dives and letting it shoot if it starts to hang back.

On the Ion I always felt like I was flying too actively. It's so damped and safe, that it does a lot of that active flying for you by damping out the dives almost completely. When I went up to the Gin Explorer (High B) I immediately felt more at home, because then my active flying suddenly worked a lot better! Hard to explain... Like my brain calculating the wing "should" dive right now and I should catch it, but on the Ion that dive never came. Same thing with the pendulum effect of you under the wing... On the Ion I tried to balance it out after a curve, but the Ion "eats" that surplus energy by itself, so you'd end up almost over-correcting.

Hi Martin,

Ive flown the Owens Valley in the US, and nowhere else in the world Ive flown over 35 years comes close in terms of consistently strong thermals and rip-roaring turbulence.

However, UK thermals can sometimes be just as turbulent. A spring day with a cold, unstable airmass, inversions, strong winds, or wave influence can all result in insanely turbulent thermals.

To illustrate Simon Blakes last point that on some days, you arent safe until your feet are on the ground and the glider is balled up, Ill relate an incident that occurred to me in the UK around 10 years ago.

I was on an XC on a cold, spring day. Conditions had generally been good - reasonably rough-edged thermals but not overly turbulent for a UK spring day. Winds were light, not a cause for concern.

An hour or so into the XC, the clouds were becoming well spaced, and Id just done a long, smooth, lightly sinky glide in search of the next thermal.

I was getting low, and the vario had remained stubbornly silent for the last few minutes. The air was smooth at this point, no cause for concern.

At about 500 feet, and with the next cloud still a little way away, it was looking like the flight was coming to an end. It was time to pick a landing field. There were plenty to choose from. I chose a large field with no obstructions, did a couple of 360s to check the wind direction and set up to land into wind.

The first unusual thing I noticed was that as I descended, the wind direction switched by 90 degrees at about 200 feet. The air was still very smooth.

It was such a large field, that wasnt a problem I just changed the plan to land in a different part of the field.

Now the ground was approaching, I dropped my legs ready for landing, and prepared myself for a light wind landing, i.e. round out and flare.

Then I noticed something unusual. Something that will be etched on my memory forever.

At about 10 feet AGL (Yes, that's TEN feet), I focused on the exact spot where I was about to touch down (as you do). What I saw was a circular movement in the grass, with a few bits of grass rushing inward and just above the ground.

I didnt have time to be concerned about this situation, as within a split second, the vario started screaming up, the ground started receding and the wing was pitching and writhing like a bucking bronco. Decades of active flying experience kicked in as I tried to keep the wing open.

But many years of active flying skill were not going to guarantee keeping the wing open in that air. I had been lifted up to maybe 80 feet when I took a large asymmetric. I was lucky, I was on a very well behaved wing a UP Trango 3 which didnt turn much even after a large collapse.

Dealing with the consequences of the collapse and regaining control in a critical situation like this requires such total focus, that there is no mental capacity left for such thoughts as how much this could hurt. However, in my peripheral vision I was aware of the ground approaching again as I lost height in the recovery process.

As the wing recovered, I hit screaming lift again, followed soon after by another large collapse. Dealt with the collapse and I was still going up.

I got to around 100 feet this time. The idea of trying to centre this beast at this height would have been complete insanity, so the instant the lift stopped I just pointed away from the thing. I landed two fields away in perfectly smooth air into a light wind. Not even a rustle in the grass, almost flat calm where I landed.

I stood there for several minutes before doing anything. I could think of nothing but that circular motion of the grass at the precise spot two fields away where I was about to land a minute ago. I had this mental image of looking down the barrel of a gun, and I had just dodged the bullet.

Im not sure if this fact is reassuring or not; but nothing like it has happened to me before, or since.

Andy

In 2013 (June 7th) I was standing on the top of Coniston Old Man in the Lake District. I'd gone up there with the intention of flying a few Wainwrights. When I got to the summit it was flat calm, no wind at all, so I settled in to wait for something to happen. A couple of other guys turned up and we nattered for a bit. One chap was on a clock, so he unpacked his wing. It was lying on the ground, laid out nicely, with his flying suit, gloves and helmet on top of it. We were standing near the summit cairn when I heard an odd noise. From where we were, I ran over to the summit cairn. Standing there, the wind was HOWLING, trouser flappingly strong. Over by where I'd been standing a matter of seconds before it was still flat calm. The chap whose glider was out had great presence of mind and ran over to his wing and grabbed and sat on a wingtip. About three seconds later it reared up like an angry cobra and started twisting around and around. His helmet, gloves and suit went in all directions, and the wing was a mess when, ten or fifteen seconds later, it settled back to the ground. He'd seen me standing in a howling wind and realised immediately that it was a symptom of an approaching dust devil. Having no dust, it was completely invisible, and we only heard it 'cos it was whistling through the summit cairn. I dread to think what would have happened if he'd been strapped in and making his run for an alpine launch when it grabbed him.

That was summertime in the Lakes, not (I would have thought) "spring conditions", a term I usually assume applies to March/April. I don't know what anyone could have done to predict the approach of the beast.

Even when the thermals are weak there are dangers especially at this time of year, that can catch you unawares.

One issue to be aware of is low level inversions also called cold pools. During the night the air cools and sinks. The lower air and higher air can separate at a defined layers. Below the air is cold, dense and blowing in one direction, above the air is warmer but can be blowing much stronger in a completely different direction. That upper warmer air just slides across the lower level like its a smooth sheet of ice and can be very strong. A pilot might take off in the morning in what appears fairly calm, stable soaring conditions. During the day the boundary between the two layers warms up due to weak thermals from below. At a certain point the temperature difference at the boundary layer become small enough that the layer can suddenly break up and the much stronger wind above breaks through from a completely different direction than the lower level wind causing a lot of sudden turbulence.

Please do not think that summer thermals maybe your only threat to safe flying.
Winter flying is a bit more tricky than summer thermic stuff because you do not often have the same cues, ie the clouds, birds,smell, noise.
The winter air is often more stratified and windows we may attempt to fly in more varied, so we can have for example cold air pooling, thermals coming off rivers/lakes built up areas, very pronounced katabatic flows, inversions, and wave.
United Kingdom and Ireland has very dynamic weather systems at the best of times and as such these moving weather systems and masses can have very pronounced differences in both distance and time.
For example I live at approx 600 feet over looking a large valley where it all looks good, go up another 400 feet and the conditions can be 15 mph stronger and even in different direction. Last year I took off a hill at 1500 west facing and was going up fairly steadily watching my ground speed reduce to zero 1000 ATO and the wind was 45 different. Thankfully I had a reasonable expectation that there was wave and did not have to much difficulty getting out of the lift and come back fairly sedately. Other times I have been dumped from 20 feet, and others have have mistaken wave for thermals and started turning in it with unpleasant consequences.
Give yourself more time to observe the conditions definitely take advice from more experienced pilots.

MartyMack wrote:
Is there any way to spot a cold pool?


The really important thing to check is what the wind is doing above the ground inversion - a cold pool in itself isn't a problem. Some days the inversion will just mean you can only soar the very top of a mountain and if you sink below the inversion, you're done because there's no wind down there. Sites like https://www.windy.com/ give easy access to higher level winds as well as forecasting what you'll feel at ground level. Check the 2000' forecast and if it's blowing a gale up there then you might want to leave the wing in the bag. If the inversion breaks, those winds will suddenly come down to lower levels and if you climb up though the inversion, you're going to get a kicking.

If you can't feel much wind on takeoff, but clouds are skipping along 1000' above you then be very cautious, especially if they're going in a different direction to what you can feel on the hill. Reading the sky on the day is important, especially if there's wave about, but the forecast will usually let you know if you need to worry about inversions and wind gradients.

Marty,
you've had lots of really good advice and examples to draw on here.

One or two things which you didn't ask about, but which may help you through your first spring.

You don't mention flying much with others, but try and find a *communicative*, mixed crowd of pilots to fly with (as in, a group with a range of experience). Hopefully they'll have a Telegram / FB group or some such and be sharing info about what's coming up and the sort of conditions to expect. If there aren't any immediately local to where you intend to fly, then make the effort to travel - being in company doubles your rate of learning ... and some.

Every thing thus far has been about external conditions, but there are "internal conditions" too - the way you react in your head to the flying conditions on any given day may vary quite a lot. Sometimes you'll feel fine with a bit of rough (for you) air; the following week, you may find yourself hating what others find to be similar conditions to the previous time.

It's not a universal truth, but your progression as a pilot - stretching the boundaries of what you are comfortable with - may not be smooth and linear, this game sometimes feels like two steps forwards and one step back. Don't fret about it, don't beat yourself up wondering why every bump felt horrible, you were got anxious and decided to land: it just happens and you did the right thing.

I can assure you, almost all of us had our share of those days early on, and even years later, it can still happen.

I kinda feel it's the wrong question ... which should be, "is it rough close to the ground in the UK"?

And because flying typically involves soaring a small 1-200m ridge for quite a lot of the flight in a moderate wind needed to stay up, that can expose the pilot to more risk of crashing into the ground without throwing the reserve, than for example, a day at Annecy. Also, the wind can stir up unpredictable "chop" and interacts with the thermals to accentuate thermic turbulence. The extent of turbulence whilst ridge soaring is to a large extent determined by the topography. Some sites have a reputation for being rough, and looking at an OS map for upwind lumps and bumps gives you some idea, but you will have a feel for that from flying in Ireland. Low level invisible dusties are not uncommon, generated from dry crop fields out front, which are probably worse than the green boggy fields of Ireland.
